# Question 014: Albuterol Sulfate/Budesonide (Airsupra)

## Drug Information

- **Generic Name**: Albuterol sulfate/budesonide
- **Trade Name**: AIRSUPRA
- **Sponsor**: Bond Avillion 2 Development LP (in conjunction with AstraZeneca)
- **MOA**: Fixed-dose combination (FDC) of a short-acting beta2-adrenergic agonist (SABA, albuterol) for rapid bronchodilation and an inhaled corticosteroid (ICS, budesonide) to treat airway inflammation, administered together as a single reliever inhaler
- **Pharmacologic Class**: SABA/ICS fixed-dose combination (first approved ICS/SABA FDC in the U.S.)
- **Formulation**: Pressurized metered dose inhaler (pMDI); albuterol 180 µg/budesonide 160 µg per dose (2 actuations of 90 µg/80 µg), as needed

## Proposed Indication

For the as-needed treatment or prevention of bronchoconstriction and to reduce the risk of exacerbations in patients with asthma 18 years of age and older.

## Target Population

- Adults ≥18 years with asthma (all severity levels; including patients across all severity levels, approximately corresponding to GINA Steps 2–5)
- Patients using as-needed reliever therapy for asthma symptoms
- All patients with asthma are vulnerable to severe exacerbations regardless of baseline asthma severity

## Line of Therapy

Reliever (rescue) therapy — as-needed use in place of SABA alone; may be used with or without background controller therapy

## Disease Background

- Asthma is a common and potentially serious chronic respiratory disease characterized by variable symptoms, airway inflammation, airway hyperresponsiveness, and bronchoconstriction. It affects an estimated 235 million people globally and has a U.S. prevalence of 8% among both children and adults.
- Asthma is a heterogeneous disease with a spectrum of phenotypes and a range of severities; however, all patients with asthma—regardless of severity—are vulnerable to severe acute exacerbations.
- Acute asthma exacerbations account for a large portion of healthcare utilization, morbidity, and mortality. Severe exacerbations may require hospitalization, prolonged or high-dose systemic corticosteroids (SCS), and may result in death; SCS use is itself associated with significant morbidity.
- The management of asthma follows a stepwise approach based on continuous assessment and treatment adjustment.

## Current Standard of Care

- **Reliever therapy (as-needed)**:
  - Short-acting beta2-agonists (SABA): albuterol (ProAir, Proventil, Ventolin), levalbuterol — the only class of inhaled medication currently approved as reliever therapy in the U.S.; no reliever treatment carries an indication to prevent severe acute exacerbations
- **Controller (maintenance) therapies** (multiple classes, stepped approach):
  - Inhaled corticosteroids (ICS): beclomethasone, budesonide, ciclesonide, fluticasone, mometasone, etc.
  - ICS/LABA combinations: budesonide/formoterol (Symbicort), fluticasone/salmeterol (Advair), mometasone/formoterol (Dulera), fluticasone/vilanterol (Breo Ellipta)
  - ICS/LABA/LAMA: fluticasone/vilanterol/umeclidinium (Trelegy Ellipta)
  - Leukotriene receptor antagonists: montelukast, zafirlukast, zileuton
  - Anticholinergics: tiotropium
  - Biologics: omalizumab (anti-IgE), mepolizumab, reslizumab, benralizumab (anti-IL-5/5R), dupilumab (anti-IL-4Rα), tezepelumab (anti-TSLP)
  - SMART therapy (single inhaler maintenance and reliever): ICS/formoterol used as both maintenance and rescue — not approved in the U.S. for reliever use
- **GINA/NAEPP guideline evolution**: Recent international (GINA 2022) and national (NAEPP EPR-4 2020) guidelines recommend using an ICS in combination with a SABA as the preferred alternative rescue treatment for patients with mild disease (Steps 1–2), in the absence of approved SMART therapy; GINA recommends this as young as 6 years of age

## Unmet Medical Need

There are no reliever treatments that carry an indication to prevent severe acute asthma exacerbations. SABA monotherapy addresses bronchoconstriction but does not treat the underlying airway inflammation that drives exacerbations. Although many controller therapies reduce exacerbations when used regularly, patients requiring rescue therapy during symptom onset receive no anti-inflammatory benefit from existing reliever options. AIRSUPRA is the first approved ICS/SABA combination product in the U.S., providing an opportunity to treat both bronchoconstriction and inflammation simultaneously at the time of symptom onset, thereby reducing exacerbation risk without requiring a change in reliever behavior.
