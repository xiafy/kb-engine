# Question 015: Relugolix (Orgovyx)

## Drug Information

- **Generic Name**: Relugolix
- **Trade Name**: Orgovyx
- **Sponsor**: Myovant Sciences, Inc.
- **Code Name**: TAK-385
- **MOA**: Oral small molecule gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH) receptor antagonist. Competitively blocks GnRH receptors in the anterior pituitary gland, preventing endogenous GnRH from inducing LH and FSH release, thereby suppressing production and secretion of testosterone from Leydig cells in the testes, resulting in medical castration
- **Pharmacologic Class**: Gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH) receptor antagonist
- **Formulation**: Oral tablet; loading dose 360 mg on Day 1 followed by 120 mg once daily

## Proposed Indication

Treatment of adult patients with advanced prostate cancer.

## Target Population

- Adults with advanced prostate cancer, including:
  - PSA biochemical relapse following primary surgical treatment or radiotherapy with curative intent
  - Newly diagnosed metastatic prostate cancer
  - Advanced localized disease for which immediate primary surgical treatment or radiotherapy is unlikely to be curative
- Requiring at least 1 year of continuous androgen deprivation therapy (ADT)

## Line of Therapy

Foundational androgen deprivation therapy (ADT); first-line systemic treatment for advanced prostate cancer; castration-sensitive disease

## Disease Background

- Prostate cancer is the second most common cancer in men worldwide, with over 1.2 million cases and 358,000 deaths annually (Bray et al. 2018). Approximately 11.6% of men in the U.S. will be diagnosed with prostate cancer during their lifetime.
- There will be an estimated 33,000 prostate cancer deaths in 2020 in the U.S., comprising approximately 5% of all cancer-related deaths. The 5-year survival rate for men with advanced or metastatic prostate cancer ranges from 26–30%.
- Prostate cancer cells are highly dependent upon androgens (testosterone, dihydrotestosterone) for growth; therefore, ADT is the foundational therapy for advanced prostate cancer and has been used for several decades.
- Patients with metastatic prostate cancer face a high risk of life-threatening complications increasing with time: skeletal-related events (spinal cord compression, vertebral collapse, pathological fractures), fatigue, frailty, bone pain, and limited quality of life.
- With improved therapies, more men are living longer, and cardiovascular disease has become the most common non-cancer cause of death in this population, now exceeding prostate cancer deaths.

## Current Standard of Care

- **Surgical castration**: Orchiectomy — rapid and reliable, but irreversible
- **Medical castration with GnRH agonists** (injectable depots, the current predominant standard):
  - Leuprolide acetate (Lupron Depot® monthly/quarterly/biannual, Eligard® approved 2002): most commonly used
  - Goserelin acetate (Zoladex®)
  - Major limitation: cause an initial testosterone surge ("flare") upon administration, which can worsen clinical symptoms (bone pain, spinal cord compression, bladder outlet obstruction) in up to 63% of patients; flare managed with concomitant antiandrogen (e.g., bicalutamide, which carries hepatotoxicity/gynecomastia risks)
  - Cardiovascular risk is higher with GnRH agonists vs. antagonists, particularly in men with pre-existing cardiovascular disease
- **Medical castration with GnRH antagonist** (injectable):
  - Degarelix (Firmagon®, approved 2008): no testosterone surge; however, limited by high injection volume, frequent injection site reactions, and required monthly injections — making it less tolerable in clinical practice
- **Key limitations of all existing ADT options**:
  - All are injectable depot formulations (no oral daily option available)
  - Long-lasting testosterone suppression persists for months after discontinuation (highly variable recovery, dependent on duration of therapy and patient age), prolonging adverse effects and precluding rapid reversal when needed (e.g., after myocardial infarction, hip fracture)
  - Persistently low testosterone causes carbohydrate/lipid metabolic abnormalities, bone mineral density loss, cardiovascular events, cognitive dysfunction, and sexual dysfunction
  - GnRH agonists carry a higher cardiovascular risk vs. GnRH antagonists in men with pre-existing cardiovascular disease

## Unmet Medical Need

Despite the availability of multiple GnRH agonist and one GnRH antagonist formulations, all current ADT options are injectable and carry significant limitations. There is no oral daily option for testosterone suppression available in the U.S. An oral GnRH antagonist that (1) rapidly suppresses testosterone without an initial surge/clinical flare, (2) avoids the injection site and inconvenience burden, (3) allows more predictable and rapid testosterone recovery upon discontinuation, and (4) has potential cardiovascular safety advantages over GnRH agonists represents a meaningful advance for patients with advanced prostate cancer.
