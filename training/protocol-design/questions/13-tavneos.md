# Question 013: Avacopan (Tavneos)

## Drug Information

- **Generic Name**: Avacopan
- **Trade Name**: TAVNEOS
- **Sponsor**: ChemoCentryx, Inc.
- **MOA**: Small molecule selective antagonist of complement component 5a receptor (C5aR). C5a is a potent neutrophil chemoattractant and agonist generated by the terminal complement cascade; proposed to play a central role in the pathogenesis of ANCA-associated vasculitis through an amplification loop of ANCA-mediated neutrophil activation
- **Pharmacologic Class**: Complement C5a receptor antagonist
- **Formulation**: 10 mg capsules, 30 mg twice daily with food

## Proposed Indication

Adjunctive treatment of adult patients with severe active antineutrophil cytoplasmic autoantibody (ANCA)-associated vasculitis (granulomatosis with polyangiitis [GPA] and microscopic polyangiitis [MPA]) in combination with standard therapy including glucocorticoids. TAVNEOS does not eliminate glucocorticoid use.

## Target Population

- Adults with severe active ANCA-associated vasculitis: GPA or MPA
- Newly diagnosed or relapsing disease
- Receiving standard induction therapy (cyclophosphamide or rituximab)
- Severe disease defined as life- or organ-threatening manifestations (e.g., rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis, diffuse alveolar hemorrhage, mesenteric ischemia, scleritis, nervous system involvement)

## Line of Therapy

First-line induction and maintenance (adjunctive to standard of care with CYC/RTX and glucocorticoids)

## Disease Background

- ANCA-associated vasculitis (AAV) is a group of systemic vasculitides affecting small and medium arteries, associated with antineutrophil cytoplasmic antibodies targeting PR3 or MPO. The incidence of AAV is approximately 20 per million per year in Europe and North America; prevalence is 200–400 cases per million.
- AAV occurs more frequently in males, between ages 60–70 years, and in White and Asian populations.
- If left untreated, AAV is uniformly fatal with mean survival <1 year, typically from respiratory and renal failure.
- With currently available treatments, remission rates reach approximately 90% and mortality has decreased to ~20% at 5 years; however, therapy-related toxicities (59%) and active vasculitis (14%) remain the most frequent early causes of death.
- Relapse rates range from 10–60%, particularly in the 12–18 months after immunosuppression is discontinued, and relapse episodes may be severe, organ- or life-threatening.

## Current Standard of Care

- **Induction therapy** (for severe GPA/MPA):
  - High-dose glucocorticoids (prednisone 45–60 mg/day based on weight, tapered over 20 weeks) in combination with:
    - Cyclophosphamide (CYC) oral 2 mg/kg/day for 3–6 months, followed by azathioprine (AZA) maintenance; OR
    - Rituximab (RTX) 375 mg/m² IV weekly × 4 doses
  - Both CYC and RTX are FDA-approved in combination with glucocorticoids for GPA and MPA
- **Maintenance therapy** (to prevent relapse):
  - Low-dose glucocorticoids combined with:
    - Azathioprine (AZA), rituximab (RTX, follow-up treatment per updated labeling 2018), methotrexate (MTX), or mycophenolate mofetil (MMF)
  - EULAR guidelines recommend at least 24 months of maintenance therapy after remission induction
  - Rituximab has become increasingly preferred for maintenance (MAINRITSAN trials)
- **Limitations**: Cyclophosphamide causes myelosuppression, infertility, malignancy, and bladder toxicity. Glucocorticoids at high doses carry substantial toxicity including infection, metabolic effects, bone loss, and cardiovascular risk.

## Unmet Medical Need

Despite high remission rates achievable with current standard of care, there remains an unmet need for therapies that can:
1. Better control disease activity in patients who do not respond to or relapse on available therapies
2. Reduce or replace glucocorticoid use, given that glucocorticoid-related toxicity (including infection, metabolic complications) accounts for 59% of early deaths in the first year
3. Provide additional therapeutic options for patients who are intolerant of cyclophosphamide or rituximab
The review acknowledges that while currently available therapies are effective at achieving remission and preventing relapse, "there remains an unmet need for additional therapeutic options for those who do not respond to currently available therapies, as well as a need for alternatives with fewer toxicities."
