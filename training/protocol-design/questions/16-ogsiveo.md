# Question 016: Nirogacestat (Ogsiveo)

## Drug Information

- **Generic Name**: Nirogacestat
- **Trade Name**: OGSIVEO
- **Sponsor**: SpringWorks Therapeutics, Inc.
- **Code Name**: PF-03084014
- **MOA**: Oral small molecule gamma-secretase inhibitor (GSI). Gamma-secretase is a multi-protein complex that cleaves the Notch intracellular domain (NICD), which acts as a transcription factor promoting cell proliferation. Inhibition of gamma-secretase reduces Notch signaling and downstream tumor cell proliferation in desmoid tumors (DTs), which are characterized by Notch pathway activation
- **Pharmacologic Class**: Gamma-secretase inhibitor
- **Formulation**: 50 mg tablet; 150 mg (3 × 50 mg tablets) orally twice daily (BID)

## Proposed Indication

For the treatment of adult patients with progressing desmoid tumors who require systemic treatment.

## Target Population

- Adults with desmoid tumors (DTs) that are progressing by radiographic assessment (RECIST) or causing clinically significant symptoms requiring systemic therapy
- Patients who require systemic treatment (i.e., not candidates for active surveillance alone)
- No prior requirement for specific prior therapy lines; DTs may be newly diagnosed or previously treated

## Line of Therapy

Systemic treatment line — for patients with desmoid tumors requiring active systemic therapy (no prior systemic therapy required; drug-naive and previously treated patients eligible)

## Disease Background

- Desmoid tumors (DTs) are a rare subtype of soft tissue sarcoma (STS) that are locally aggressive and characterized by infiltrative growth that can affect surrounding vital organs, causing significant morbidity: chronic pain, functional deficits, psychological problems, and decreased quality of life.
- DTs account for approximately 0.03% of all tumors (Reitamo 1982) and are diagnosed in approximately 900–1,500 patients in the U.S. annually, making them a rare condition.
- DTs occur between ages 15 and 60 years, with a peak incidence around age 30 years, and are twice as common in females as in males.
- On the molecular level, DTs are characterized by activating mutations in the β-catenin gene (CTNNB1) or the adenomatous polyposis coli gene (APC). Most DTs arise sporadically; 5–15% are associated with familial adenomatous polyposis (FAP) caused by APC mutations.
- DTs do not metastasize but can be life-threatening through local invasion (particularly intra-abdominal tumors causing intestinal obstruction). Survival: approximately 96% alive at 5 years, 92% at 10 years, and 87% at 15 years; outcomes depend on tumor site, size, and proximity to vital structures.
- Although most tumors grow progressively, growth is often indolent and periods of growth arrest or spontaneous regression are not uncommon.

## Current Standard of Care

There is **no FDA-approved therapy** for desmoid tumors at the time of submission. Treatment guidelines (NCCN v1.2023) reference the following options without an established standard of care:

- **Active surveillance ("watch and wait")**: Appropriate initial approach for asymptomatic/mildly symptomatic patients, especially given propensity for spontaneous regression. The sorafenib placebo arm showed a 20% spontaneous response rate, highlighting the importance of observation.
- **Surgery**: Offered to selected patients with resectable extra-abdominal or abdominal wall DTs; however, recurrence rates range from 19–77% with various factors affecting recurrence. Complete surgical resection is often challenging due to infiltrative growth.
- **Radiation therapy (RT)**: Appropriate for patients who need local treatment but are not surgical candidates, or for whom surgical morbidity would be excessive; risk of radiation-associated malignancies is a concern, especially in young patients.
- **Systemic therapies** (no consensus standard of care; evidence largely from case series and retrospective analyses):
  - NSAIDs (e.g., sulindac)
  - Hormonal therapy (e.g., tamoxifen)
  - Conventional cytotoxic chemotherapy (e.g., doxorubicin-based regimens, methotrexate/vinblastine)
  - Tyrosine kinase inhibitors (e.g., sorafenib, imatinib, pazopanib): active in some patients but unapproved for DT
  - All systemic agents used off-label with variable efficacy and toxicity

## Unmet Medical Need

There are no FDA-approved therapies for desmoid tumors. Due to the rare and locally aggressive nature of DTs, complete surgical resection is often challenging and associated with high recurrence rates (up to 70% in some series). Radiation therapy reduces recurrence when combined with surgery, but given the high rate of abdominal tumors and long-term radiation toxicities (including secondary malignancies), its use has important limitations. For systemic therapies, there is no consensus on which agent to use, at what dose, or for how long; data are largely from uncontrolled, small retrospective studies. Patients with progressing desmoid tumors requiring systemic treatment represent a population with high unmet medical need, and nirogacestat is the first drug to receive FDA approval for this indication.
