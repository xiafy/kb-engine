# Question 012: Deucravacitinib (Sotyktu)

## Drug Information

- **Generic Name**: Deucravacitinib
- **Trade Name**: Sotyktu
- **Sponsor**: Bristol-Myers Squibb Company
- **Code Name**: BMS-986165
- **MOA**: Selective tyrosine kinase 2 (TYK2) inhibitor; binds to the pseudokinase (regulatory) domain of TYK2, uniquely stabilizing an autoinhibitory interaction between the pseudokinase and catalytic domains, thereby allosterically inhibiting TYK2 kinase activity
- **Pharmacologic Class**: Tyrosine kinase 2 (TYK2) inhibitor
- **Formulation**: 6 mg oral tablet

## Proposed Indication

Treatment of adults with moderate-to-severe plaque psoriasis who are candidates for systemic therapy or phototherapy.

## Target Population

- Adults with moderate-to-severe plaque psoriasis
- Eligible for systemic therapy or phototherapy
- Baseline disease severity: Static Physician's Global Assessment (sPGA) score ≥3 ("moderate") on a 5-point scale, Psoriasis Area and Severity Index (PASI) score ≥12, and ≥10% body surface area (BSA) involvement

## Line of Therapy

Systemic treatment line (includes patients who have and have not received prior systemic therapy)

## Disease Background

- Psoriasis is a common, chronic, immune-mediated, inflammatory skin disorder with a strong hereditary component. The prevalence of psoriasis in the U.S. is approximately 2–3%, of which an estimated 20% have moderate-to-severe disease.
- Psoriasis can first appear at any age, with two peaks in onset: ages 20–30 years and 50–60 years.
- The most common form is chronic plaque psoriasis (psoriasis vulgaris), characterized by sharply demarcated scaly erythematous plaques on extensor surfaces.
- Psoriasis is considered part of a multisystem inflammatory disorder associated with metabolic syndrome, cardiovascular disease (atherosclerosis, myocardial infarction), psoriatic arthritis (affecting ~one third of patients), inflammatory bowel disease, and psychiatric/psychological disorders (depression, anxiety, suicidal ideation).
- The natural history is chronic with intermittent remissions; the disease results in substantial impairment of quality of life.

## Current Standard of Care

- **Topical therapy**: First approach for limited disease; topical corticosteroids, vitamin D analogs, retinoids
- **Phototherapy**: UVB (narrowband or broadband) or PUVA (UVA + methoxsalen); limited by office-based administration requirements and need for photoprotection
- **Conventional systemic agents**:
  - Methotrexate (approved 1972): effective but has teratogenic, hepatotoxic, nephrotoxic effects and bone marrow toxicity/pulmonary fibrosis risk
  - Cyclosporine: restricted indication (severe, recalcitrant disease) due to nephrotoxicity and hypertension
  - Acitretin: teratogenicity and hyperlipidemia are labeled risks
  - Apremilast (PDE4 inhibitor): more benign safety profile but limited efficacy (PGA clear/almost clear ~16–18%)
- **Biologic therapies** (all injectable):
  - Anti-TNF agents: adalimumab, etanercept, infliximab, certolizumab; boxed warning for serious infections, lymphoma, malignancy
  - IL-17A blockers: secukinumab, ixekizumab
  - IL-17 Receptor A antagonist: brodalumab (additional boxed warning for suicidal ideation and behavior)
  - IL-12/23 blocker: ustekinumab; labeled for malignancy and posterior leukoencephalopathy
  - IL-23 inhibitors: guselkumab, tildrakizumab, risankizumab
  - Anti-IL-17, -IL-12/23, and -IL-23 products demonstrate PGA clear/almost clear rates of 55–88%

## Unmet Medical Need

Despite multiple approved therapies, there remains an unmet need for additional treatment options. No currently available therapy provides a permanent cure or universal response. Treatment is frequently complicated by inadequate response, loss of response, adverse reactions, and comorbid conditions. All biologic products may be associated with immunogenicity and loss of effect. Patients who fail or are intolerant to existing systemic therapies, including biologics, need additional options. As an oral small molecule with a novel TYK2 target distinct from JAK1/2/3 inhibitors and biologics, deucravacitinib addresses this gap. The availability of an oral agent may also benefit patients who prefer or require a non-injectable route of administration.
