# Question 018: Vorasidenib (Voranigo)

## Drug Information

- **Generic Name**: Vorasidenib
- **Trade Name**: Voranigo
- **Sponsor**: Servier Pharmaceuticals, LLC
- **Code Name**: AG-881, S095032
- **MOA**: Orally bioavailable small molecule inhibitor of mutant isocitrate dehydrogenase-1 (IDH1) and IDH2 enzymes; inhibits neomorphic IDH enzymatic activity, reducing production of the oncometabolite 2-hydroxyglutarate (2-HG); designed with CNS penetration
- **Pharmacologic Class**: IDH1 and IDH2 inhibitor
- **Formulation**: Tablets: 10 mg and 40 mg

## Proposed Indication

Treatment of adult and pediatric patients 12 years and older with Grade 2 astrocytoma or oligodendroglioma with a susceptible IDH1 or IDH2 mutation following surgery including biopsy, sub-total resection, or gross total resection.

## Target Population

- Adults and pediatric patients ≥12 years of age
- Histologically confirmed WHO Grade 2 astrocytoma or oligodendroglioma
- IDH1 or IDH2 mutation (susceptible mutation)
- Prior surgery (biopsy, sub-total resection, or gross total resection)
- Post-operative observation setting; no requirement for prior chemotherapy or radiation

## Line of Therapy

Post-surgical (observation-replacing); adjuvant setting following initial surgery for newly diagnosed or recurrent Grade 2 IDH-mutant glioma

## Disease Background

- Gliomas are the most common form of primary malignant brain tumors, arising from glial cells in the CNS; approximately 3,500 new cases of Grade 2 diffuse glioma (low-grade glioma, LGG) occur annually in the United States.
- IDH1 mutations are present in approximately 80% of Grade 2 and 3 diffuse gliomas; IDH2 mutations in approximately 4%; IDH mutations occur early in tumorigenesis and are disease-defining characteristics of diffuse gliomas (Cohen et al, 2013).
- The median age of diagnosis is 38 years for astrocytomas and 45 years for oligodendrogliomas; there is a slight male predominance and higher incidence in White patients; these young patients often have no significant comorbidities but suffer from seizures, cognitive decline, and other neurological symptoms.
- LGGs are considered incurable and carry a long but ultimately fatal natural history: reported median OS for Grade 2 LGGs is 10–12 years; malignant transformation (MT) into high-grade glioma occurs in 23–72% of patients with a median time of 2.7–5.4 years (Murphy et al, 2018).
- IDH mutations lead to accumulation of the oncometabolite 2-HG, resulting in widespread epigenetic disruption, altered DNA methylation, impaired cellular differentiation, and tumor immune microenvironment changes.

## Current Standard of Care

- **Low-risk Grade 2 LGG** (age ≤40 years, gross total resection):
  - Surgery followed by active observation with serial MRI (deferred treatment approach to delay toxicity of RT/chemotherapy)
- **High-risk Grade 2 LGG** (age >40 years, or subtotal resection/biopsy, large tumor, neurological deficits):
  - Surgery followed by radiotherapy (RT) and adjuvant chemotherapy; preferred regimens: procarbazine/lomustine/vincristine (PCV) or temozolomide (TMZ)
  - ORR with PCV or TMZ ranges from 30–69% in prospective studies
- **Recurrent or higher-grade disease**:
  - Surgery, re-irradiation, chemotherapy, and clinical trials
  - No approved therapies specifically for IDH-mutant LGG at any disease stage prior to vorasidenib approval

## Unmet Medical Need

There are no FDA-approved therapies specifically targeting IDH-mutant Grade 2 astrocytoma or oligodendroglioma. Available treatments (chemotherapy, RT) are not curative, are associated with substantial short- and long-term toxicities (neurotoxicity, radiation necrosis, leukoencephalopathy, cognitive deficits, treatment-induced hypermutation), and do not target the IDH mutation directly. Active observation in low-risk patients defers treatment but does not prevent malignant transformation. For young patients facing decades of disease, there is a substantial unmet need for targeted, better-tolerated therapies that can delay progression and malignant transformation while preserving cognitive function and quality of life.
